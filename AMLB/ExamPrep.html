Big Picture

The goal of the basic module exam is not to test whether you memorized isolated definitions. It is much closer to checking whether you understand the methodology, the logic of machine learning workflows, and the meaning of the choices made throughout the course.

If you understand why a method is used, when it should be used, and what can go wrong, you are preparing in the right way.

This means that your revision should focus less on recalling sentences word for word and more on being able to recognize the right concept in a realistic situation. A lot of questions are best approached by asking yourself: “What problem is this method trying to solve?”

What the Written Exam Feels Like

The written part is designed as a 15-question multiple-choice test. The point is not to reward memorization, but to check whether you can recognize the right methodological choice in a realistic machine learning situation.

Core format
  • 15 multiple-choice questions
  • 4 options per question
  • reasoning-focused rather than coding-focused
  • more methodology than calculation
What matters most
  • understanding workflow order
  • choosing the right metric or split
  • recognizing near-correct distractors
  • spotting common methodological mistakes
The written exam is much more about understanding why a method is used than about recalling a definition word for word.

How to Study Efficiently

The best preparation for this written test is not to memorize isolated facts. It is to understand each concept well enough to recognize it in a question, distinguish it from similar concepts, and explain why it is the right choice.

Step 1

Rebuild the course as a conceptual map, not only as a sequence of notebooks.

Step 2

For each topic, ask: what is it, when is it used, and what is it often confused with?

Step 3

Practice explaining why one answer is correct and why the others are close but wrong.

concept → distinction → use case → correct choice

If you can define a concept in your own words, contrast it with a related one, and explain why it applies in a given situation, then you are preparing in the right direction for this exam.

Highest-Priority Topics

If your time is limited, prioritize the concepts that are both central to the course and easy to confuse during a multiple-choice exam.

Priority topic What you should understand
Overfitting vs underfitting What each means, how they appear, and how regularization or model complexity affects them.
Bias vs variance Why they are different and how they relate to model behavior and diagnostics.
Train / validation / test Why the roles of these sets are different and how mixing them creates leakage.
Precision, recall, F1, ROC AUC What each metric measures and when one is more informative than another.
EDA and cleaning Why data understanding comes before modeling and what kinds of issues EDA is meant to reveal.
Scaling and preparation Why scaling matters, what it changes, and how it differs from cleaning.
Cross-validation and resampling Why resampling exists, what changes across methods, and why K-fold is often the default choice.
Model selection and tuning Why choosing an algorithm and tuning its hyperparameters are related but distinct decisions.

What You Should Be Able to Explain

A good way to check whether you are really ready is to see whether you can explain the following distinctions clearly, without relying on memorized wording.

Core distinctions
  • Overfitting vs underfitting
  • Bias vs variance
  • Precision vs recall
  • Accuracy vs Log Loss
  • Train vs validation vs test
Methodological choices
  • Why regularization helps
  • Why accuracy can fail on skewed classes
  • Why cross-validation is useful
  • Why scaling can matter for some models
  • Why data leakage is dangerous
If you can explain not only what these ideas are, but also what they are often confused with, you are much closer to the kind of understanding the written exam rewards.

Common Mistakes

The easiest way to lose points is not by forgetting everything, but by confusing two concepts that sound related.

Accuracy vs confidence

Accuracy only counts correct predictions. It does not tell you how certain the classifier was.

Train vs test contamination

If test information influences training, the evaluation becomes unrealistically optimistic.

Bias vs variance

High bias means the model is too simple. High variance means it adapts too much to the training data.

Metric mismatch

Using the wrong metric for the problem can make a model look better than it really is.

Resampling confusion

Static split, K-fold, LOOCV, and repeated splits are related, but they differ in reliability and computational cost.

Model choice vs tuning

Choosing a model family is not the same as optimizing its hyperparameters.

Multiple-Choice Strategy

In this exam, the wrong answers are often not absurd. They are often close to the right idea, but wrong in stage, purpose, or interpretation.

What to do first

  • identify the topic immediately
  • ask what problem the question is really about
  • check which option matches the workflow stage

What to watch out for

  • an answer that is generally true, but wrong here
  • an answer that belongs to another step of the pipeline
  • an answer that sounds technical but solves a different problem
A very common trap is the almost-right answer: correct concept, wrong use case.

If two options look similar, the correct one is usually the one that fits the context more precisely. In many questions, the difference is not the definition itself, but whether the concept is being applied at the right stage and for the right reason.

Last-Minute Checklist

If your exam is close, this is the strongest final pass for the written part.

Final revision priorities
  • Lecture 04: regularization, bias–variance, train/validation/test
  • Lecture 05A: precision, recall, F1, ROC AUC
  • Lecture 06–07: EDA, cleaning, scaling, feature preparation
  • Lecture 08: resampling, metrics, comparison, tuning
Five questions to be able to answer
  • Why can accuracy be misleading?
  • Why do we need train/validation/test separation?
  • What is the difference between high bias and high variance?
  • When should I use K-fold cross-validation?
  • What is the difference between Grid Search and Randomized Search?
For a 15-question written exam, clarity beats breadth. It is better to understand the core methodological choices well than to skim every detail once more.
